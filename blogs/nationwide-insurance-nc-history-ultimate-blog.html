The Farm Mutual Origins: Born from Necessity, Built on Trust

The Nationwide story begins not in a corporate boardroom, but in the fields and farmhouses of rural Ohio in 1926. At that time, farmers across America faced a serious problem: the major insurance companies of the day either refused to insure farm properties or charged prohibitively expensive rates.

Murray D. Lincoln, a visionary leader in the Ohio Farm Bureau Federation, recognized this crisis. Farmers were the backbone of America, yet they couldn't get affordable, reliable insurance protection for their homes, barns, livestock, and equipment. So Lincoln did something revolutionary—he created an insurance company owned by and for farmers.

The Birth of Farm Bureau Mutual Automobile Insurance Company

On April 17, 1926, Farm Bureau Mutual Automobile Insurance Company was founded with a simple but powerful mission: provide quality, affordable automobile insurance to Ohio farmers and their families. The initial investment? Just $10,000. The first policy? Written for farmer Raymond P. Cook.

What made this company different from day one was its mutual structure. Unlike stock insurance companies that answer to distant shareholders, Farm Bureau Mutual was owned by its policyholders. This meant every decision was made with customers' best interests at heart, not Wall Street's profit demands.

Expanding Beyond Ohio: Nationwide Comes to North Carolina

The company's early success with Ohio farmers proved that the model worked. Within just a few years, Farm Bureau Mutual expanded beyond auto insurance to offer fire insurance for homes (1934) and life insurance (1935). But the truly transformative moment came in 1955.

The Name Change That Changed Everything

In 1955, the company made a strategic decision that would define its future: it changed its name to Nationwide Insurance. Why? Because the company had grown far beyond its Ohio farm roots and was now serving families across America—including North Carolina.

The name "Nationwide" wasn't just marketing—it was a promise. A promise that the same values that protected Ohio farmers would now protect families from coast to coast. And for North Carolina, this expansion would prove to be a game-changer.

Why North Carolina Was a Natural Fit

North Carolina in the 1950s and 60s shared many characteristics with Nationwide's original Ohio customer base:

  • Strong agricultural heritage: NC was (and still is) a major agricultural state with tobacco farms, dairy operations, livestock, and diverse crop production
  • Rural communities: Much of North Carolina remained rural, with families living on larger properties that standard insurance companies found difficult to insure
  • Underserved market: Many NC families struggled to find affordable, comprehensive insurance that understood their unique needs
  • Values alignment: North Carolinians valued the same things Nationwide was built on—honesty, community, and taking care of your neighbor

  • Livestock and animal mortality insurance

Understanding NC's Rural Character

Despite our growing cities, much of North Carolina remains rural. Nationwide understands properties that other carriers find challenging:

  • Homes on larger acreage (10, 20, 50+ acres)
  • Properties with private wells and septic systems
  • Homes with gravel driveways
  • Older homes with historical significance
  • Properties with wood-burning stoves or heat sources
  • Dwellings not visible from the road

North Carolina Insurance Requirements

If you're shopping for Nationwide insurance in NC, here's what you need to know about state requirements:

  • Minimum auto liability: $30,000/$60,000 bodily injury, $25,000 property damage (Nationwide recommends higher limits)
  • Uninsured motorist coverage: Required in NC and essential given 7-8% of drivers are uninsured
  • Homeowners insurance: Not required by law, but mandatory if you have a mortgage
  • Flood insurance: Separate from homeowners; Nationwide can help arrange through NFIP
