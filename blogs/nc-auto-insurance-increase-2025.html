Good News for Riders!

If you've received a renewal notice recently and wondered why your car insurance went up, you're not alone. As of October 1, 2025, North Carolina auto insurance rates have increased by an average of 5% statewide. This affects every driver renewing or purchasing new auto policies in the Tar Heel State.

But before you panic — let me give you some perspective. Insurance companies actually asked for a 22.6% increase (later bumped to 23%). Thanks to Insurance Commissioner Mike Causey's negotiations, we're only seeing 5%. That's a savings of nearly 18 percentage points for NC drivers.

What Happened: The Official Details

On June 9, 2025, North Carolina Insurance Commissioner Mike Causey announced a settlement with the NC Rate Bureau — the organization that represents insurance companies writing auto policies in our state. Here's what the settlement includes:

Settlement Details

  • Auto Insurance: 5% average statewide increase (originally requested: 22.6%)
  • Motorcycle Liability: 16.3% average statewide DECREASE (great news for riders!)
  • Effective Date: October 1, 2025 for new and renewed policies
  • Duration: Rates remain in effect until October 1, 2026
"With factors such as distracted driving, excessive speeding, and increased automobile repair costs putting upward pressure on insurance rates, I am happy that we were able to hold the average increase to 5%."
— Mike Causey, NC Insurance Commissioner

Why Did NC Auto Insurance Rates Go Up?

The 5% increase didn't come out of nowhere. Several factors have been putting upward pressure on insurance costs across North Carolina:

1. Distracted Driving Epidemic

Commissioner Causey has consistently pointed to distracted driving as the #1 cause of accidents — and therefore rate increases — in North Carolina. We've all seen it: drivers texting, checking social media, or watching videos while behind the wheel. Every accident caused by distraction drives up costs for everyone.

2. Excessive Speeding

Speed-related crashes have been on the rise since the pandemic. Faster speeds mean more severe accidents, higher repair bills, and more expensive claims. I-77, US-421, and even local roads in Surry County have seen their share of speed-related incidents.

3. Higher Vehicle Repair Costs

Modern vehicles are essentially computers on wheels. According to the NC Rate Bureau, today's cars have sensors in bumpers, mirrors, headlights, and windshields — components most likely to be damaged even in minor collisions. A simple fender-bender that used to cost $500 to fix can now run $2,000 or more.

Hurricane Helene Impact

The NC Rate Bureau also cited losses from nearly 23,000 totaled vehicles due to devastating flooding from Hurricane Helene in autumn 2024. These catastrophic losses affect rates statewide.

4. Rising Vehicle Replacement Costs

Whether it's a new car or used, prices have surged since 2020. When it costs more to replace a totaled vehicle, insurance companies need more money to cover those claims.

7 Proven Ways to Offset the Rate Increase

A 5% increase doesn't have to mean paying more. Here are concrete strategies I help clients with every day:

1. Shop Your Policy — Seriously

This is the #1 most effective way to save. Different carriers rate risks differently, and a rate increase is the perfect excuse to compare. I've seen clients save 15-30% by switching — more than enough to offset the increase.

2. Bundle Home and Auto

Most carriers offer 10-25% discounts when you combine your auto and home (or renters) insurance. If you haven't bundled yet, now's the time.

3. Review Your Deductibles

Raising your collision and comprehensive deductibles from $500 to $1,000 can reduce your premium by 15-20%. Just make sure you can afford the higher out-of-pocket if you do have a claim.

4. Ask About All Available Discounts

You'd be surprised what you might qualify for: good driver discounts, low mileage discounts, anti-theft device discounts, defensive driving course discounts, multi-vehicle discounts, good student discounts, and more. Ask your agent to review everything.

5. Improve Your Credit (Yes, It Matters)

In North Carolina, your credit-based insurance score affects your premium. Paying down debt and maintaining good credit can meaningfully lower your rates.

6. Consider Usage-Based Insurance

Programs like Progressive's Snapshot or Nationwide's SmartRide track your driving habits. Safe drivers can save up to 30% based on how they actually drive.

7. Drop Unnecessary Coverage (Carefully)

If you have an older vehicle worth less than $5,000, you might consider dropping collision and comprehensive coverage. Use my 10% Rule Calculator to see if this makes sense for you.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does the 5% increase apply to everyone equally?

No. The 5% is a statewide average. Your actual increase may be higher or lower depending on your driving record, the type of coverage you have, your vehicle, and other factors. Drivers with accidents or violations typically see larger increases, while those with clean records may see smaller ones.

When will I see this increase on my policy?

The increase applies to policies that begin or renew on or after October 1, 2025. If your policy renewed in September 2025 or earlier, you won't see this increase until your next renewal date.

Why are motorcycle rates going down while auto rates go up?

Motorcycle claims data in North Carolina has shown improving trends, while auto claims have worsened due to distracted driving and higher repair costs. The Rate Bureau adjusts each line of insurance independently based on claims experience.

Can I switch insurance companies to avoid the increase?

Yes and no. The rate increase applies to all companies writing auto insurance in NC. However, different carriers price risk differently, so shopping around may still save you money — or at least offset the increase. I regularly help clients find savings by comparing multiple carriers.

Will rates keep going up every year?

Unfortunately, recent trends suggest continued pressure on rates nationally. However, NC's unique regulatory process helps moderate increases. The next rate filing is due February 1, 2026. Whether rates increase again will depend on claims trends, repair costs, and driving behavior across the state.

Does this affect the minimum liability requirements?

No, this rate change doesn't affect NC's minimum liability requirements. However, note that NC's minimum limits are increasing in July 2025 due to Senate Bill 452 passed in 2023. The new minimums will be 50/100/25 (up from 30/60/25), which may affect your premium separately.
