NC Traffic Laws Most Drivers Get Wrong

Let's break down the traffic laws that trip up even experienced NC drivers. Understanding these can save you from expensive tickets and insurance rate increases.

The Move-Over Law (Most Violated)

North Carolina's Move-Over Law requires drivers to move over one lane OR slow down to at least 10 mph below the speed limit when passing stopped emergency vehicles, utility vehicles, or tow trucks with flashing lights. The fine? $250+ and points on your license. Since July 2025, this law has been more strictly enforced with increased penalties.

School Bus Stop Rules

NC has some of the strictest school bus laws in the nation. When a school bus displays its stop sign, ALL traffic in BOTH directions must stopâ€”unless there's a physical median dividing the highway. The penalty: up to $500 fine and 5 points on your license for a first offense.

Right Turn on Red

While right turns on red are generally legal in NC after a complete stop, many drivers don't realize you must yield to ALL pedestrians and trafficâ€”not just oncoming vehicles. Additionally, some intersections prohibit right turns on red entirely, and missing the sign is no defense.

Did You Know?

A single moving violation in NC can increase your insurance rates by 15-30% for 3-5 years. That "minor" $100 ticket could cost you $1,000+ in higher premiums.

Headlight Requirements

NC law requires headlights to be on from sunset to sunrise, but also during rain, fog, smoke, or any other conditions that reduce visibility to 400 feet or less. Many drivers don't realize that "daytime running lights" don't countâ€”you need full headlights that also illuminate your taillights.

NC Insurance Rules That Surprise Most Drivers

North Carolina has unique insurance requirements that differ from most other states. Here's what you need to know:

July 2025 Insurance Law Changes

As of July 1, 2025, NC significantly increased minimum liability limits and now requires Uninsured/Underinsured Motorist (UM/UIM) coverage to be offered with every policy. While you can still reject UM/UIM coverage in writing, the new minimums are:

  • $50,000 bodily injury per person
  • $100,000 bodily injury per accident
  • $50,000 property damage per accident

These limits nearly doubled from the previous $30,000/$60,000/$25,000 requirements. However, with NC's high accident rates and medical costs, I still recommend at least $100,000/$300,000/$100,000 coverage. The cost difference is often just $100-200 per year for significantly better protection.

NC's Safe Driver Incentive Plan (SDIP)

Unlike most states, NC uses a point system that directly affects your insurance rates. Points stay on your record for 3 years and can increase your premiums significantly:

  • 1 point: Minor speeding (10 mph or less over) = ~25% rate increase
  • 2 points: Speeding 10+ mph over = ~45% rate increase
  • 4 points: At-fault accident = ~65% rate increase
  • 12 points: DWI = ~400% rate increase (if insurable at all)

Prayer for Judgment Continued (PJC)

NC's unique "Prayer for Judgment Continued" allows you to plead guilty to a traffic offense while asking the court not to enter judgment. When granted, this keeps the offense off your driving record for insurance purposes (with limitations). You can use one PJC every 3 years per household for insurance purposesâ€”but only for certain offenses.

NC Is an "At-Fault" State

North Carolina is one of only a few "pure contributory negligence" states. This means if you're even 1% at fault for an accident, you cannot recover damages from the other driver's insurance. This makes proper coverage limits absolutely critical.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long do points stay on my NC driving record?

Points stay on your NC driving record for 3 years from the date of convictionâ€”not the date of the violation. However, your insurance company may consider violations for up to 5 years when calculating your rates. After 3 years, the points no longer affect your license status, but the conviction remains visible on your record.

Can I take a defensive driving course to reduce my insurance rates in NC?

Unlike some states, NC doesn't mandate insurance discounts for defensive driving courses. However, some insurance carriers voluntarily offer discounts of 5-15% for completing approved courses. Additionally, if you're over 55, NC law requires insurers to offer a discount for completing an approved mature driver course. Ask your agent about specific carrier discounts.

What happens if I'm caught driving without insurance in NC?

NC takes uninsured driving seriously. First offense penalties include: $50-$100 fine, license plate surrender, vehicle registration revocation, and a $50 restoration fee. Your vehicle cannot be legally driven until you provide proof of insurance to the DMV. Repeat offenses carry higher fines and longer suspension periods.

Does NC require me to report accidents to my insurance company?

While NC law doesn't require you to report accidents to your insurer, your policy almost certainly does. Most policies require prompt notification of any accidentâ€”even if you don't plan to file a claim. Failing to report can give your insurer grounds to deny coverage if the other party later makes a claim against you. When in doubt, report it.

What's the difference between collision and comprehensive coverage?

Collision coverage pays for damage to your vehicle when it hits another object (car, tree, guardrail) regardless of fault. Comprehensive covers non-collision damage: theft, vandalism, weather, fire, hitting a deer, and falling objects. Neither is required by NC law, but lenders require both if you're financing. For older vehicles, these may not be worth the premium.
